Father's Day Message

A Man's Calling

The Lord's Prayer as a Blueprint for Fatherhood

Bishop Darlingston Johnson • Harvest Intercontinental Church-Olney

Luke 11:2 • June 21, 2026

A Day to Honor the Fathers

We paused the series on the Word of God — because today is Father's Day. We've come to honor the men of this house for the great work they do and the great responsibility they shoulder.

Some of us had great fathers. Others had fathers who were absent, distant, and never knew how to pass down the craft. So we set out to father, to lead, to build a family — but we have no pattern to follow.

Men Are Called to Build

Buy furniture from Ikea with no blueprint and it can be a disaster — you finish, realize you did it wrong, and have to take it apart and start again.

We Are Builders

As men we are building families, homes, ministries, communities. And to build well, we need a blueprint.

The Blueprint Jesus Gives

"When you pray, say: Our Father in heaven, hallowed be Your name. Your kingdom come. Your will be done on earth as it is in heaven."

Luke 11:2 (NKJV)

Jesus gives us more than a manual for prayer — He gives us a pattern for fatherhood: the blueprint of the Heavenly Father Himself.

The Elder Brother Teaching the Younger

Jesus, the firstborn — the patterned Son who knew the Father long before we came along — is like an elder brother teaching his younger brothers about the kind of Father they have.

"You don't have to be a spectator — men and women, take this and apply it to your life."

Part 1

"Lord, Teach Us to Pray"

The first lesson every man must learn

The Most Important Skill

The wisdom you need, you don't have. The strength you have is not enough. The job is too important.

The most important lesson a man can master is to learn how to pray — because you cannot accomplish the task God has given you without God's help.

"When You Pray" — Not "If"

Notice Jesus said when you pray, not if you pray. Prayer is not optional. And it is not enough to know how to pray — you need to actually pray.

"You still don't know how to pray until you pray."

The Help Is Already There

"Let us therefore come boldly to the throne of grace, that we may obtain mercy and find grace to help in time of need."

Hebrews 4:16 (NKJV)

"If any of you lacks wisdom, let him ask of God… and it will be given to him."

James 1:5 (NKJV)

The Legacy of a Praying Father

The Greatest Inheritance

One of the greatest legacies you will leave your children is the memory of a father they saw pray — and who taught them to pray.

Teach them by word and example. Let them hear your testimonies of answered prayer — and invite them to pray with you.

Part 2

"Our Father"

The great revelation of the New Covenant

The Name God Chose

The Old Testament reveals God by powerful names — Jehovah Rapha, Jehovah Jireh, Emmanuel. They are good and true. But the revelation of God as Father was not fully developed in the Old Covenant.

When Jesus came to establish the New Covenant, the name He pressed us to grasp was not Jehovah, not the Almighty, not the Great Judge — it was Father. "Into Your hands… Father, not My will but Yours."

Why That Name Matters

If God — King of kings, Creator of the universe — chooses to be known as Father, then in God's own mind the role of fathering is the role that best matches who He is and what He does for us.

"You are never more like God than when you are fathering."

Called to Co-Father With God

A Spiritual Calling, Not Just Biological

When God calls men "fathers," He is calling us to be His instruments — co-workers providing the fathering role that is essential for every human being. Every man, with or without biological children, is called to father.

Receive it not as a weight to feel guilty about, but as honor: "Lord, who am I that You are mindful of me?"

The Heart of It

Three Needs a Father Meets

Unmet, they are a cage — met, the bird flies

Three Foundational Needs

1. Physical Security

To feel safe, protected, provided for.

2. Emotional Security

To be loved unconditionally — not for performance, but for whose we are.

3. Significance

To know "I matter. I am needed. I exist for a purpose."

Physical Security

Until there is enough food, until I know the rebels won't come in the night with guns — the moon can wait. A people without safety cannot dream of the stars.

"When my father came home, I felt safe. Once he walked in, the fear was gone."

Emotional Security — Unconditional Love

Every one of us needs to feel loved — not because of what we do. It is not our performance that determines how much we are loved. We are accepted because of whose we are.

When a person embraces "I am loved and accepted regardless of my performance," they are set free.

Significance — Like Yourself, Not Just Love Yourself

We were all born loving ourselves — that can make us selfish. But it is one thing to love yourself; it is another to like yourself.

Many love themselves yet don't like themselves — and it wounds them deeply. A father helps a child arrive at: "I matter. I am needed. And I like me."

From Self-Actualization to Christ-Actualization

The Bird Out of the Cage

When safety, love, and significance are met and no longer weighing us down, we are freed — not to actualize ourselves, but to be conformed to the image of Jesus.

"That's what it means to go to the moon, to go to the stars — we become more and more like Christ."

No One Is Better Positioned

As important as the mother is, as important as siblings are — no one occupies a higher position to meet these three needs than those of us called to father, biological or spiritual.

How a man conducts himself has a far greater impact on how a child experiences safety, love, and significance — and that frees them to pursue Christ-likeness.

Part 3

"Which Art in Heaven"

Present • Available • Accessible • Approachable

He Knew Where to Find His Dad

"In heaven" is not a statement of distance — it is a statement of availability. Jesus knew where to find His Father. His Father wasn't missing; He was present, available, accessible, approachable.

King of kings, running the universe — yet always present in His Son's life. "When I need His voice, I can get it. When I need correction, He gives me wisdom."

The Memory That Brought Him Home

"And he arose and came to his father. But when he was still a great way off, his father saw him and had compassion, and ran and fell on his neck and kissed him."

Luke 15:20 (NKJV)

After all his rebellion, it was the memory of his father that gave him courage to rise and go home. "Even though I left him, he didn't leave me — and I know where to find him."

Presence, Not Just Presents

P-R-E-S-E-N-T-S

Thank God for what we provide — but it is not the deepest need.

P-R-E-S-E-N-C-E

You can live in the same house and still not be there. Be present emotionally, not just physically.

The Bishop's confession: his wife in the kitchen, and he never lifted his head. "Don't be an absentee father in your own home."

Part 4

"Hallowed Be Thy Name"

Your name is your reputation — and your legacy

The Name Is the Character

"Name" here is not "Darlingston" — it is reputation and character. When our Father's name is mentioned, it is connected to faithfulness, love, goodness, holiness, mercy, kindness.

"When you squeeze Him, nothing comes out but goodness, mercy, kindness, love, and joy."

Leave a Name They Can Be Proud Of

Live so that when your name is mentioned in your absence — and your child happens to be in the room — what it evokes is gratitude, love, and pride. Not because you were rich or held office, but because of your character.

Your greatest legacy is not material — it is the name you leave in them by how you lived before them. Young people: start now.

The God of Restoration

"I've Already Messed Up My Name"

Thank God He is the God of restoration. You can go back to your children — or whoever you lead — humble yourself, and say: "I was wrong. I'm sorry. With God's help, I'm going to do better."

That humble acknowledgment is itself part of the legacy you leave. Guard your name for the rest of your days.

Part 5

"Thy Kingdom Come"

Every father is a king

Every Man Is a King

Not a king of a country or a palace — but a king because a king has authority over a sphere of influence. People look to you, listen to you, follow you.

Every man is a king — not big "K," but small "k" — over a domain where what you say and do has tremendous impact.

What Kind of Kingdom Are You Building?

The question is not whether you are a king, or whether you have authority — you do. The question is: what are you doing with it? What is the culture of the kingdom you are building?

The culture of our homes — love, peace, joy, harmony, a place that glorifies God — depends largely on what we say and do.

A Servant-King

"Your kingdom come. Your will be done on earth as it is in heaven."

Luke 11:2 (NKJV)

The kingdoms of the world seek to be served. But the Father's kingdom is one where the king serves the people — providing order, protection, and a culture where his wife, children, and those he leads can flourish. Let your kingdom be a microcosm of His.

Part 6

"Thy Will Be Done"

Every king needs a vision

Where Are You Leading Them?

If your wife and children say, "Father, let your will be done" — they are choosing to follow you. So where will you lead them?

  • To a place of peace and prosperity?
  • To a place where they know God?
  • To embrace the kingdom and the will of God?

A Vision of Heaven on Earth

That is the kind of Father our Heavenly Father is. He has a plan, a will, a destination. "Your kingdom come on earth" — He has a vision of earth being just like heaven, and He leads us there.

Because we trust where He is taking us, we don't resist — we embrace. Cast a vision so good that those who follow you want to come.

So We Go Back to God

Now that we know the honor, the calling, and our own limitations — what do we do? We go back to the Father and seek His help at every step. God is not angry; He is saying, "Guys, I need you. Let's work together."

"You don't have to be perfect to be a good father. You just need to be committed to the pursuit."

Committed to the Pursuit

"Therefore you shall be perfect, just as your Father in heaven is perfect."

Matthew 5:48 (NKJV)

He would not command it without giving us what we need to become it. So be honest when you fall short — "I'm sorry, but I haven't given up; I'll keep trusting Jesus to conform me to His image until we get it right."

Respond

Prayer Points

For the men who answer the calling

  • Praying men — that every man would learn to pray and actually pray, coming boldly to the throne of grace (Hebrews 4:16).
  • The revelation of God as Father — that men would grasp the honor of co-fathering with God and receive wisdom for it (James 1:5).
  • True presence — break the power of distraction; make us PRESENCE, not just PRESENTS, in our homes.

Prayer Points 1 of 3

Prayer Points (2 of 3)

  • Meeting the three needs — that fathers would make their homes places of security, unconditional love, and significance, freeing children for Christ-likeness (Romans 8:29).
  • A godly name — that every man's reputation would carry faithfulness, integrity, and love as a legacy for his children.
  • Restoration — for fathers who have failed or been absent, grace to humble themselves, make it right, and begin again (Luke 15:20).

Prayer Points 2 of 3

Prayer Points (3 of 3)

  • Servant-kings — that men would build a kingdom culture in their homes that mirrors heaven (Luke 11:2).
  • Godly vision — that fathers would know where they are leading those who follow them, to peace, prosperity, and the knowledge of God.
  • Committed to the pursuit — not perfection, but a refusal to quit; keep returning to the throne of grace.

Prayer Points 3 of 3

Declaration

A Father's Confession

"Father, thank You for the privilege to be a man — a father, a husband, a leader like You. We are honored. Yet we are conscious of our lack. So we turn to You for the grace to become what You want us to be. We commit not to perfection, but to the pursuit."

In Jesus' name. Amen.

Study Notes

Sermon Summary

A Man's Calling — Bishop Darlingston Johnson

Key points, principles, and takeaways

Overview & Key Scriptures

Overview — A Father's Day message that uses the Lord's Prayer as a phrase-by-phrase blueprint for godly manhood. God's deliberate choice to reveal Himself as "Father" in the New Covenant is itself a declaration of how indispensable fathering is — and no man can fulfill it without learning to pray and actually praying.

Luke 11:2 — the core text; each phrase ("Our Father… hallowed be Your name… Your kingdom come… Your will be done") unlocks a dimension of what the Father is like, and therefore what men are called to be.

Hebrews 4:16 — come boldly to the throne of grace; the help a man needs is already there, accessed through prayer.

James 1:5 — the wisdom of fathering is not innate; if you lack it, ask.

Luke 15:20 — the prodigal's father: present, accessible, unconditional. The memory of that father drew the son home.

Matthew 5:48 — "be perfect as your Father is perfect": not a crushing standard but a promise of provision.

Summary 1 of 5

Main Teachings

Teach us to pray — a man must know how to pray, actually pray, and leave a legacy of prayer. "When you pray," not "if."

Our Father — the name God chose for the New Covenant; fathering is the role God Himself plays. Men are called to co-father with Him — a spiritual, not merely biological, calling.

Three needs — physical security, emotional security/unconditional love, and significance. Met, they free a person for "Christ-actualization." Fathers are uniquely positioned to meet them.

Which art in heaven — God is present, available, accessible, approachable (the prodigal son). Be PRESENCE, not just PRESENTS.

Hallowed be Thy name — your name is your reputation; leave your children a name they can be proud of; restoration is available.

Thy kingdom come — every father is a king (small "k"); be a servant-king building a kingdom that mirrors heaven.

Thy will be done — every king needs a vision; lead those who follow you to peace and the knowledge of God.

Summary 2 of 5

Principles

Prayer is the prerequisite of fatherhood — no man fulfills the calling without God's help; the access point is prayer.

God's self-identification reveals the calling's dignity — choosing "Father" as His name elevates fathering to the highest spiritual rank.

Presence is more powerful than provision — relational presence outweighs material presents.

Unconditional love creates the conditions for return — the prodigal came back because of a remembered love.

A man's name is his most valuable legacy — character outlasts wealth and position.

The culture of a kingdom flows from its king — the home's atmosphere is shaped by the man's words and actions.

Failure is not final — the God of the blueprint is also the God of restoration. Christ-likeness, not self-actualization, is the goal.

Summary 3 of 5

Warnings & Cautions

Absentee fathering — "you can live in the same house and still not be there." Busyness, even legitimate work, can be emotional absence that wounds.

Knowing without praying — theological knowledge of prayer is not prayer; the help comes only by actually coming to the throne.

Receiving the calling as a burden — do not let it become guilt or condemnation; embrace it as the highest honor.

Neglecting your name — "when they Google your name, don't let them find that you were arrested." Build a name now.

Building a self-serving kingdom — the temptation of every king is tyranny; the call is to the servant-king.

Leading without vision — authority without direction carries people somewhere by default.

Letting past failure define the future — guilt must not paralyze renewed commitment.

Summary 4 of 5

Takeaways & Declarations

Practical takeaways — pray "Lord, teach me to pray" daily; keep a consistent prayer place and pray visibly before your children; audit your emotional presence; evaluate the culture of your home; articulate a Christ-centered vision for your family; guard your name in every interaction; if you have failed, humbly make it right; come boldly to the throne for wisdom; embrace the calling as honor. The closing homework: "Go to God in prayer and really, really pray about this."

Declarations — "Thank You for the privilege of co-fathering with You." "Lord, help us to be praying fathers, praying leaders, praying men." "Let my kingdom be a microcosm of Your kingdom; let my kingship reflect the kind of King You are." "With Your help I will do better — I trust Jesus to keep working in me to make me the father I should have been." "We commit not to perfection, but to the pursuit."

Summary 5 of 5

Grace Giving · 2 Corinthians 8

Abundance In Unlikely Places

Bishop Darlingston Johnson

2 Corinthians 8:1–5 · The Macedonian Model of Generosity

Harvest Intercontinental Church, Olney · June 1, 2026

A Famine in Jerusalem

A famine has struck Jerusalem and the believers there are suffering — not enough food. Word reaches Paul, and he launches a campaign across the churches to raise relief for the body of Christ in need.

The Corinthians had pledged — but stalled

They promised to give, but it has been a while and the pledge is unfulfilled. Paul writes, sending Titus: "Guys, go ahead and complete what you promised — it is really to your advantage to do so."

Two Whole Chapters on One Offering

In 2 Corinthians, Paul spends two full chapters — chapters 8 and 9 — on this single offering. Some feel a pastor should never talk about money. But Paul will not be silent, because the stakes are too high.

Scripture speaks about money more than twice as much as any other subject — Jesus referenced it constantly, because He understood the role it plays in our lives.

Not the Devil — Money

"No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve God and money."

Matthew 6:24 (NKJV)

The devil is not the one competing with God for first place in your life — money is. Besides God, money is the most powerful force at work in the world. There is no sin in that — we just must not let it replace God.

He Appeals to Grace, Not Law

What Paul did NOT do

He did not say, "The law commands 10% — give or God will judge you." He did not pull apostolic rank: "Do it because I said so."

What Paul DID do

He appealed to the grace of God — and let the Holy Spirit lift up another church entirely: the Macedonians.

God's Chosen Model

The Spirit instructs Paul to hold up the Macedonian church as a model for the Corinthians to look at, imitate, and follow. "God made the Macedonians the model for the Corinthians."

And because it is in Scripture, they become the model for us too — so we can learn what pleases the Father, in His churches, in His people, in me, in you, in this church.

Could God Point to Us?

"My prayer is that the day will come, if it isn't here yet, when the Father can say: just go over there to 16227, Olney, Maryland — when He is looking for a church, or for believers, to point to as an example."

The greatest ambition

You may aspire to be CEO of your company — that's fine. But the greatest aspiration is to be like Jesus. "In everything, in every way, and in this matter of giving, I want to be like Jesus."

Part 1

Things That Don't Belong Together

2 Corinthians 8:1–2 — the verse that stopped the Bishop

v1–2 — The Grace Bestowed

"Moreover, brethren, we make known to you the grace of God bestowed on the churches of Macedonia: that in a great trial of affliction the abundance of their joy and their deep poverty abounded in the riches of their liberality."

2 Corinthians 8:1–2 (NKJV)

"This time it really struck me — there are things in that verse that shouldn't go together."

Affliction + Joy

A great trial of affliction — intense, severe. Not "I had a bad day." They were persecuted: beaten, jailed, some even killed. What belongs with great affliction is great sorrow.

Yet coupled with it: an abundance of joy. That is not normal. The two do not naturally fit.

Deep Poverty + Liberality

Not minimum-wage poverty. Not "don't know where the next bill is coming from." This is rock-bottom, ocean-deep poverty — "so poor that the poor people called us poor."

That level of poverty naturally produces a scarcity mindset — conserve, preserve, hold on. Yet here it is coupled with an abundance of liberality. They don't go together.

This Looks Suspiciously Supernatural

How can you face such affliction and still express such joy? How can you be so poor and still be so generous?

"This is not natural. This is not usual. This looks suspiciously supernatural."

The Source: The Grace of God

"...the grace of God bestowed on the churches of Macedonia."

2 Corinthians 8:1 (NKJV)

There is where it comes from. The joy and the generosity are located in the grace of God — not in their circumstances. "If I allow grace to truly work in me, this is the effect it produces, independent of my circumstances. Whether I have a lot or nothing, I can be joyous and generous."

Declaration

When grace enters the picture

"When the grace of God enters the picture, it is possible to experience abundance of joy and radical generosity — in spite of the circumstances."

Part 2

How God Measures Generosity

The heart, not the hand

Heart, Not Hand

There is a definition of generosity that is not tied to how much you give. When God measures generosity, He does not measure what is in your hand — the size of your offering.

He measures what is in your heart. What determines your generosity is not how much you gave, but how much you wanted to give.

Largest Givers ≠ Most Generous Givers

We thank God for our largest givers — some give forty, fifty thousand a year, and may God increase their capacity. But hear this carefully:

"Our largest givers are not necessarily our most generous givers in the sight of God." Because God looks not at the amount, but at the heart behind it.

The $100 Heart

"For if there is first a willing mind, it is accepted according to what one has, and not according to what he does not have."

2 Corinthians 8:12 (NKJV)

You give $100 — all you can — praying, "God, I wish I had a thousand to give." All you gave was a hundred, but God saw how much you wanted to give. That is the basis for whether you are generous.

While the Economy Is Economizing

Maybe your circumstances changed and you cannot give what you once gave. "But guess what — while the economy is economizing, your level of generosity does not have to decrease."

Your prayer can be: "God, I'm giving this, but You know I want to give more — make that possible." That is being generous in God's sight.

The Widow's Mite

"...this poor widow has put in more than all... for she out of her poverty put in all that she had, her whole livelihood."

Mark 12:43–44 · cf. Luke 21:1–4 (NKJV)

Jesus stopped the whole service. The others gave out of abundance — good. But she gave all. "Here is the Macedonian — she didn't give a lot, but she gave all." How much does your heart want to give?

Why Do You Want Increase?

"Let this mind be in you which was also in Christ Jesus."

Philippians 2:5 (NKJV)

When you pray for promotion, salary, business growth — what is the motive? Don't let it be limited to your own needs. Let your desire for increase be, in part, a holy longing to be positioned by God as a greater blessing to His kingdom. "If that is your prayer, God is listening."

Part 3

According To — and Beyond — Ability

2 Corinthians 8:3–4 — the call to sacrifice

v3 — Freely Willing

"For I bear witness that according to their ability, yes, and beyond their ability, they were freely willing."

2 Corinthians 8:3 (NKJV)

They had little ability — yet grace moved them past the limit of what they could spare.

No Command — Only Love

"I am not commanding you," Paul says. "With God it is accepted according to what a man is willing to do. I don't want you to give because you feel forced."

"Let your giving be because of love — let it prove the sincerity of your love." Give because of love, not because of law.

Normal Giving vs. Sacrificial Giving

According to your ability

The normal, ordinary way — motivated by love, give as much as you possibly can. Nothing wrong with it; this is the standard.

Beyond your ability

When a kingdom need is so urgent it requires sacrifice — you give away not what you can spare, but what you actually need. And you suffer for it.

The One Car

A missionary needs a vehicle. Funds are raised. But someone says: "I have one car — the only one I have — and I'm going to give it to the missionary. I'll take the bus through the winter cold; I'll walk to the store, until the Lord blesses me with another."

"The need of the kingdom supersedes my convenience — I'm going to go beyond my ability." May grace work in us so that, when the moment comes, nobody has to beg us.

Part 4

The Privilege of Giving

2 Corinthians 8:4 — they begged to give

v4 — They Begged to Give

"...imploring us with much urgency that we would receive the gift and the fellowship of the ministering to the saints."

2 Corinthians 8:4 (NKJV)

Paul was reluctant to take from people so poor — "we ought to be receiving an offering for y'all." But they pleaded: "No, Paul, don't do that to us. Show us — let us participate."

When People Beg, Pastors Go Quiet

"Usually it's the pastor begging the people. The reason pastors talk about money so much is because you're holding on so tightly. I don't see you come and say, Bishop, please let us take care of this."

"If you all would plead with us more, we would be quiet. That's what grace had done in their hearts — made them willing, made them joyful."

Giving Is Grace at Work

The word translated "gift" in verse 4 is charis — grace. Paul uses "grace" some ten times across chapters 8 and 9.

The offering is a grace gift

Under the Old Testament, giving was law — something you had to do, or else. But the revelation of grace had changed their view: they no longer saw giving as losing — they saw it as joining something.

A Transfer of Grace, Not Currency

Lay hands on the sick and healing flows — that healing is grace flowing through you. Speak a Spirit-given word and it builds someone up — that word is grace through you. In the same way, when you give your finances to meet a need:

"Giving is no longer a transfer of currency — it becomes a transfer of grace, from God, through you, to meet the need."

From Obligation to Privilege

Now you understand why they were begging. "They're not pleading for an obligation — they're pleading for a privilege."

"Give us this privilege — let us partner with God in transferring grace to people, to meet the needs of His work." Is that your desire? To be a channel of grace gifts?

Part 5

The First Gift — Yourself

2 Corinthians 8:5 — the secret of the Macedonians

v5 — They First Gave Themselves

"And not only as we had hoped, but they first gave themselves to the Lord, and then to us by the will of God."

2 Corinthians 8:5 (NKJV)

Two givings. First, they gave themselves to the Lord. Then they gave themselves to the work of God. This is the secret to their sacrifice, their joy, and their liberality.

What God Really Wants Is You

"Here's the absolute truth: what God really wants is not the money in your wallet. What God really wants is what's in your heart. What God really wants is you."

A man who truly gives himself to God discovers that every other kind of giving becomes possible. "If this level of giving seems impossible, perhaps it's because you have not yet first given yourself to the Lord."

The Question That Changes Everything

The old question

"What should I do with my money?"

The new question

"Lord, what do You want to do with me?" — in this affliction, in this economy, in this moment. No matter what's around you, there are things God wants to do with you.

Make a Demand Upon Grace

"For it is God who works in you both to will and to do for His good pleasure."

Philippians 2:13 (NKJV)

Believe it. Make a demand upon it. Pray on the basis of it: "Grace of God, work in me both to will and to do Your good pleasure — in the same manner that You worked in the Macedonians."

Respond

Prayer Points

Praying the grace of the Macedonians into our hearts

Grace & Surrender

  • For transforming grace — Father, bestow on us the same grace You gave Macedonia, that we may have joy in affliction and generosity in lack, independent of our circumstances (2 Cor 8:1–2).
  • For full surrender first — we give You ourselves before our resources; turn the ownership question into the surrender question: "Lord, what do You want to do with me?" (2 Cor 8:5; Phil 2:5).

Will & Witness

  • For the will and the doing — work in us both to will and to do; change our desires, not just our behavior, and grant increase matched to a heart that longs to give (Phil 2:13; 2 Cor 9:8).
  • For our church to be a model — make us a Macedonian church others are sent to: known for begging the privilege to give, not needing to be persuaded (2 Cor 8:1; Matt 6:24).

Sacrifice & Reward

  • For readiness beyond ability — prepare our hearts now, so when extraordinary kingdom need arises we respond willingly, joyfully, without being begged (2 Cor 8:3–4).
  • For the heart God measures — thank You that You weigh desire, not amount; translate the generosity of our hearts into the capacity of our hands (Mark 12:44; 2 Cor 8:12).

Lordship Over Mammon

  • Against the spirit of mammon — no one can serve two masters; we renounce the scarcity mindset and every trust in reserves over Your provision (Matt 6:24; 1 Tim 6:10).
  • We declare: in this house, God is first — not money. Money is a tool for kingdom purposes, and Jesus alone is the Master we serve.
Declaration

When Grace Captures the Heart

"When grace truly captures the heart, generosity ceases to be a duty to perform — it becomes a privilege to embrace."

"Don't let this just be a word that goes into your ear. Make a decision. Embrace the grace of God, and start looking for opportunities to give — according to your ability, beyond your ability. Keep asking: Lord, what do You want to do with me, with my gifts, with my finances, with my time?"

Study Notes

Sermon Summary

Abundance In Unlikely Places — Bishop Darlingston Johnson

Key points, principles, and takeaways

Overview

Bishop Johnson presents the Macedonian church — a congregation enduring severe persecution and rock-bottom poverty — as God's chosen model for New Covenant giving, drawn from Paul's two-chapter appeal in 2 Corinthians 8–9 to complete the relief offering for famine-struck Jerusalem.

The central message: genuine, radical generosity is not produced by favorable circumstances or financial capacity — it is produced entirely by the grace of God working in the heart. When grace truly captures the heart, generosity ceases to be a duty to perform and becomes a privilege to embrace.

Summary 1 of 8

Key Scriptures

2 Corinthians 8:1–5 — primary text. The grace bestowed on Macedonia; deep poverty abounding in liberality; giving beyond ability; begging the privilege; they first gave themselves to the Lord.

2 Corinthians 9:8 — "God is able to make all grace abound to you, that you may have an abundance for every good work." The same grace is sufficient in every believer.

Matthew 6:24 — "You cannot serve God and money." It is money, not the devil, that competes with God for first place.

Mark 12:41–44 / Luke 21:1–4 — the widow's mite; God measures the heart, not the size of the offering.

Philippians 2:5 — "Let this mind be in you which was also in Christ Jesus." Desire increase to be a greater blessing to the kingdom.

Philippians 2:13 — "It is God who works in you both to will and to do." Make a demand upon grace.

Summary 2 of 8

Main Teachings (1–4)

1. The impossible made possible — great affliction with abundance of joy, and deep poverty with riches of liberality. These contradictions are not natural; "this looks suspiciously supernatural."

2. Grace is the source, not circumstances — everything flows from "the grace of God bestowed on the churches of Macedonia." The same reservoir is available to every believer.

3. Heart, not hand — God measures how much you want to give, not the amount. The largest givers are not necessarily the most generous in His sight.

4. According to ability vs. beyond ability — normal giving is according to ability, motivated by love; but extraordinary kingdom need calls for sacrificial giving that costs you what you actually need.

Summary 3 of 8

Main Teachings (5–8)

5. A transfer of grace, not currency — charis appears some ten times in chapters 8–9. Giving to a need is grace flowing from God through the giver; it is fellowship (koinonia) with God.

6. They first gave themselves to the Lord (v5) — what God really wants is not your wallet but you. Self-surrender first makes every other giving possible.

7. The question that changes everything — not "What should I do with my money?" but "Lord, what do You want to do with me?"

8. They begged for the privilege — in a reversal of the usual dynamic, the poor Macedonians pleaded to give. Grace turns giving from obligation into privilege.

Summary 4 of 8

Principles

• Grace produces what circumstances cannot — joy in affliction, generosity in poverty.

• God doesn't measure what's in your hand; He measures what's in your heart.

• The largest givers are not necessarily the most generous givers.

• Giving according to ability is normal; giving beyond ability is sacrificial — both are valid.

• Giving is a transfer of grace; the giver is a channel.

• The first and greatest gift is giving yourself to the Lord.

• The question of money is ultimately a question of lordship.

• When grace captures the heart, generosity becomes a privilege, not a duty.

Summary 5 of 8

Warnings & Cautions

Against law-based giving — God accepts giving according to what a person is willing to do; coerced giving misses the grace dynamic entirely.

Against letting the "economizing economy" shrink generosity — the scarcity instinct is natural, not spiritual; grace sustains generosity independent of income.

Against self-only motives for prosperity — praying for increase is not wrong, but kingdom-motivated desire for increase is the standard.

Against treating the Macedonians as "too extreme" — they were not special; it was the grace they allowed to work in them, and that grace is available to you.

Against resisting the message — reluctance signals a heart holding on rather than surrendering; the prescription is prayer for grace, not self-effort.

Summary 6 of 8

Practical Takeaways

1. Pray specifically for the Macedonian grace to work in your own heart and mind.

2. Examine what motivates your desire for increase — include a longing to give more.

3. Give according to your ability, motivated by love — not law or guilt.

4. Prepare your heart now to give beyond your ability when extraordinary need arises.

5. Reframe every gift: "I'm partnering with God to transfer His grace to a real need."

6. If giving feels impossible, go back to the first gift — give yourself to the Lord.

7. Keep asking, "Lord, what do You want to do with me?"

8. Look for opportunities to give — don't wait to be appealed to.

Summary 7 of 8

Prophetic Words & Declarations

Congregational confession — "When the grace of God enters the picture, it is possible to experience abundance of joy and radical generosity in spite of the circumstances."

A demand upon grace — "Grace of God, work in me both to will and to do Your good pleasure, in the same manner that You worked in the Macedonians."

Intercession over those raising hands — "Father, bring the kind of increase that gives them ability to give at the level You know they really want to give, in the name of Jesus."

Over the church — that the day will come when the Father can point to this church and to its believers as the example of Christ-like generosity.

Closing declaration — "When grace truly captures the heart, generosity ceases to be a duty to perform — it becomes a privilege to embrace."

Summary 8 of 8

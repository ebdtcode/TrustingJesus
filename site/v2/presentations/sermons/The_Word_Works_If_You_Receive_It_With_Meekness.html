If You Receive It With Meekness

The Word Doesn't Need More Power — It Needs Less Resistance

Bishop Darlingston Johnson • Harvest Intercontinental Church-Olney

James 1:21 • Numbers 12:3 • Colossians 3:16 • July 12, 2026

The Power Is in the Word

God's Word contains the power to reproduce, fulfill, and bring to pass everything God has ordained for you. All the blessings — everything you need for life and for godliness — is contained in the Word of God.

Like a Forest in a Seed

Just as a forest is compressed into a seed, in God's promise is contained the power to create the life that God has ordained for you.

Four Soils — One Missing Ingredient

Closed — hard; the word could not enter. What was missing? Meekness.
Casual — superficial relationship with the seed. What was missing? Meekness.
Crowded — too many things occupying the heart. What was missing? Meekness.
Cultivated — tremendous fruit. What must be present so the word can grow? Meekness.

James Provides the Answer

"So then, my beloved brethren, let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath; for the wrath of man does not produce the righteousness of God. Therefore lay aside all filthiness and overflow of wickedness, and receive with meekness the implanted word, which is able to save your souls."

James 1:19-21 (NKJV)

What do I need in my heart so the implanted word can do its work? Meekness.

The First Mention of Meekness

Numbers 12 is the first time the word "meekness" appears in the English Bible. Theologically, the first time a word is mentioned carries great significance that guides how we interpret it wherever else it appears.

So We Study Moses

If we really want to know what God — and James — have in mind when they say "meekness," we study the first mention: Numbers 12:1-3.

They Spoke Against Moses

"Then Miriam and Aaron spoke against Moses because of the Ethiopian woman whom he had married; for he had married an Ethiopian woman."

Numbers 12:1 (NKJV)

His own sister and brother — Miriam was like a mother to him. Many times those who speak against you are those who are closest to you. They had a problem with him marrying an Ethiopian woman — prejudice and racism didn't just start; they questioned his judgment.

Questioning His Right to Lead

"Has the LORD indeed spoken only through Moses? Has He not spoken through us also?"

Numbers 12:2 (NKJV)

Aaron was the high priest; Miriam prophesied. Both were anointed and called — but not appointed to Moses' role. Just like in the body of Christ: we're all called, all gifted, all anointed — but there are roles, and we must respect the roles God has assigned.

God Hears

"Don't say anything about anybody that you don't want God to hear you say."

They spoke behind Moses' back — but before their face or behind their back, God hears. And some things we say are serious enough to evoke discipline from the Lord. Be careful what you say about people — and to people.

The Meekest Man on Earth

"Now the man Moses was very humble [meek], more than all men who were on the face of the earth."

Numbers 12:3 (NKJV)

This is not merely a compliment — it is an explanation: why could Aaron and Miriam talk like that and still remain in leadership? Because Moses was meek. Had he not been, his response would have been very different.

What Moses Did Not Do

  • He did not react. Meekness.
  • He did not become angry. Meekness.
  • He did not threaten them. Meekness.
  • He did not curse them like some leaders would. Meekness.

Most people wielding Moses' authority and power would not have tolerated it. Many times, we react. Moses didn't.

Confidence, Not Self-Defense

Because of his meekness, Moses did not feel he had to defend himself or retaliate. He had enough confidence in God to leave it with God while he kept doing what he was called to do — lead Israel to the Promised Land.

Meekness at Work

A man who receives criticism and stays calm; who responds from a place of love and understanding; whose confidence in the God who called him means he never has to fight back.

"Suddenly" — God Defends the Meek

"Suddenly the LORD said to Moses, Aaron, and Miriam, 'Come out, you three, to the tabernacle of meeting!' … Then the LORD came down in the pillar of cloud… and called Aaron and Miriam."

Numbers 12:4-5 (NKJV)

God heard, and enough was enough — like a parent saying "Come, sit here; we're going to have a conversation." Notice who God called forward: Aaron and Miriam — not Moses. Moses stayed quiet, and God did the defending.

Discipline in God's House

Division among the three key leaders would have seriously affected the work — this was not something God could overlook. Miriam became leprous; it seems she was the instigator, the ringleader, and she should have known better.

Thank God He doesn't deal with us that way every time — "you'd have a leprous minister and a bunch of leprous members this morning."

"For whom the LORD loves He chastens… no chastening seems to be joyful for the present, but painful."

Hebrews 12:6, 11 (NKJV)

Try Fixing It

When Miriam was struck, Aaron — the high priest! — did not run to God. He ran to Moses: "Moses, my lord, please…" Aaron knew God would not hear him until he made it right with his brother first.

"Leave your gift there before the altar, and go your way. First be reconciled to your brother, and then come and offer your gift."

Matthew 5:24 (NKJV)

And Moses prayed for her — no cursing, no rejoicing. "Bless those who curse you… pray for your enemies." That's what meekness causes you to do.

Meekness Is Not Weakness

And meekness is not never getting angry — there is righteous indignation: "Be angry, and sin not" (Ephesians 4:26). What God forbids is reaction-anger: my ego is threatened, my importance is threatened — and I erupt. That anger "does not produce the righteousness of God."

Was Moses Weak?

  • He picked up a snake by the tail — because God said so
  • He stood before Pharaoh — a dictator with an army — and demanded: let God's people go
  • He stretched forth his rod — and the Red Sea parted
  • He led over three million constant complainers for forty years ("Manna? What's this?")

No — Moses was not weak at all.

What Meekness Is

Strength under submission

Power under control — not the absence of power.

The opposite of pride

Humility; a willingness to surrender to God.

Yielding to authority

Trusting God enough to leave the fight with Him.

Say It With Me

"Pride always has another argument.
Meekness always has another surrender."

Another excuse, another reason not to do what the Lord is saying — that's pride. Meekness surrenders.

Two Pictures of Meekness

Fire in the Fireplace

A fire hot enough to burn down the house — kept in the fireplace. "I could do real damage right now. But all of this emotion stays right here."

The Bridled War Horse

A strong, powerful animal full of energy — and with a little pull of the rein, the entire animal changes direction.

Three Faces of Aggression

Open

We retaliate openly — we want everybody to know we're angry.

Passive

We avoid them, pass by without speaking — still aggression, just passive.

Behind the Back

We hide it from them but still want to do damage.

All three are anger because the ego was bruised. That's not meekness.

Meekness in Practice

"Let every man be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath."

James 1:19 (NKJV)

The meek person is eager to listen and understand. The proud person already has his answer while you're still talking — loading up ammunition to fire back. He interrupts; he must have the last word. "I'm not perfect at this — ask my wife." And that attitude, brought to the Word, means the Word cannot penetrate.

Hear Both Sides First

"He who answers a matter before he hears it, it is folly and shame to him."

Proverbs 18:13 (NKJV)

A judge renders no verdict until he has heard the prosecution and the defense. The reason we often get angry: we've only heard the prosecution — or we are the prosecutor, wanting to be judge and jury too. A verdict from one side is likely unjust, unmerited, and unfair.

You Don't Fight for an Inheritance

"Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth."

Matthew 5:5 (NKJV)

Meek people do not have to fight for their blessings — they inherit them. It is to your advantage to clothe yourself with meekness.

One Small Word: "Let"

"Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly…"

Colossians 3:16 (NKJV)

How do we cultivate meekness toward the Word? Focus on that three-letter word. Letting is not hard. Letting simply means: stop resisting. Get out of the way.

Meekness Produces Rest

"Come to Me, all you who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest. Take My yoke upon you and learn from Me, for I am meek and lowly in heart, and you will find rest for your souls."

Matthew 11:28-29 (NKJV)

If Christianity feels hard, it's because we haven't put on meekness. Jesus offers rest, and that rest is connected to meekness: meekness produces rest; pride produces stress.

Say It With Me

"The Word doesn't need more power —
it needs less resistance."

All the power is already in the Word. God is not calling you to be stronger — He's calling you to be softer. Recognize the power that is in the Word, and just let the Word have its way.

Stop Arguing With the Word

The Word comes with authority — forgive, love, give, serve. Pride resists it: "It doesn't apply to me — she needs to forgive. I didn't do anything wrong." Arguing, excusing, defending — that is what makes life hard and keeps the word from producing fruit.

Meekness Answers: "Okay, Let's Do It"

The word says forgive? Okay, Lord, let's do it — release the power of forgiveness now. The word says give? Let's release generosity and see what it does. The word says stop worrying and pray? Let's pray — and let the power in that word produce its results.

Prayer Points

Receiving the Word with Meekness

1. A Cultivated Heart

Prayer Focus

Father, cultivate my heart into good ground. I lay aside all filthiness and overflow of wickedness, and I receive with meekness the implanted word, which is able to save my soul. (James 1:21)

2. Guard My Mouth

Prayer Focus

Lord, set a guard over my mouth. I will not say anything about anybody — to their face or behind their back — that I don't want You to hear me say. (Psalm 141:3)

3. Let God Defend Me

Prayer Focus

Like Moses, I refuse to retaliate, defend myself, or react in anger when I am criticized or spoken against. I have enough confidence in You, Lord, to leave it with You and keep doing what You called me to do. (Numbers 12:3)

4. Swift to Hear

Prayer Focus

Make me swift to hear, slow to speak, and slow to wrath. Before I judge, let me truly listen — to both sides, with an open mind — instead of loading ammunition while others speak. (James 1:19; Proverbs 18:13)

5. Meekness at Home

Prayer Focus

Lord, give my marriage and my relationships a good dose of meekness. Where anything stands between me and another person, I will go and try to fix it first — then bring my gift to You. (Matthew 5:23-24)

6. Let the Word

Prayer Focus

I stop arguing with Your Word. I stop making excuses and defending myself against it. The word says forgive, give, serve, pray — okay, Lord, let's do it. Let the word of Christ dwell in me richly. (Colossians 3:16)

Study Notes

Sermon Summary

If You Receive It With Meekness — Bishop Darlingston Johnson

Key points, principles, and takeaways

Overview

Continuing the Word Works series on the Parable of the Sower, this message answers the question the four soils leave behind: what does it take for your heart to be good ground where the all-powerful Word can take root and produce? James 1:21 supplies the answer in a single word — meekness. Meekness is what was missing from the closed, casual, and crowded hearts alike.

Using Numbers 12 — the first mention of meekness in the English Bible — Bishop Johnson holds up Moses as the portrait: a man powerful enough to confront Pharaoh and part the Red Sea, yet so meek he refused to defend himself when his own brother and sister attacked his marriage and his authority — and God defended him instead. Meekness is strength under submission, and the sermon lands on Colossians 3:16: our part is simply to let the word dwell in us richly, because the Word doesn't need more power — it needs less resistance.

Summary 1 of 7

Key Scriptures (I)

James 1:19-21 — "Be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to wrath… receive with meekness the implanted word, which is able to save your souls." The anchor text: meekness is the heart-condition the implanted word requires, and verses 19-20 describe what meekness looks like in practice.

Numbers 12:1-13 — Miriam and Aaron speak against Moses over his Ethiopian wife and question his right to lead; verse 3 declares Moses "very meek, more than all men on the face of the earth" — not a compliment but the explanation for his non-retaliation. God suddenly summons the three, defends Moses Himself, disciplines Miriam with leprosy, and restores her when Moses prays.

Colossians 3:16 — "Let the word of Christ dwell in you richly." The whole Christian posture compressed into the three-letter word "let": stop resisting, get out of the way.

Matthew 5:5 — "Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth." You don't fight for an inheritance — the meek don't have to fight for their blessings.

Summary 2 of 7

Key Scriptures (II)

Matthew 11:28-30 — "Learn from Me, for I am meek and lowly in heart, and you will find rest." Jesus connects rest to meekness: if Christianity feels hard, it's because we haven't put on meekness.

Ephesians 4:26 — "Be angry, and sin not." Meekness does not forbid all anger; righteous indignation exists. What it excludes is ego-driven reaction-anger, which "does not produce the righteousness of God."

Matthew 5:23-24 — leave your gift at the altar and be reconciled first. Aaron ran to Moses before God would hear him — "try fixing it."

Proverbs 18:13 — he who answers a matter before he hears it — folly and shame. The courtroom principle: no verdict until both prosecution and defense are heard.

Hebrews 12:6, 11 — whom the Lord loves He disciplines, and discipline is painful — the lens for Miriam's leprosy and for some of our own painful seasons.

Luke 6:27-28 — bless those who curse you; pray for those who mistreat you — exactly what Moses did for Miriam.

Summary 3 of 7

Main Teachings (I)

Meekness is the missing ingredient — the closed, casual, and crowded hearts each lacked the same thing. The one quality that turns any soil into cultivated ground for the Word is meekness.

The law of first mention — the first appearance of a word in Scripture carries interpretive weight; Numbers 12 therefore defines biblical meekness through Moses.

Attacks often come from those closest — Miriam (like a mother to Moses) and Aaron attacked both his judgment (his Ethiopian wife — prejudice is ancient) and his God-given authority ("Has the Lord spoken only through Moses?").

Calling is not the same as role — Aaron and Miriam were genuinely anointed, but anointing did not equal Moses' appointment. In the body of Christ we are all called and gifted, but we must respect the roles God has assigned.

Numbers 12:3 is an explanation, not a compliment — the verse explains why the attackers could remain in leadership: Moses did not react, rage, threaten, or curse. Most leaders with his power would have.

Summary 4 of 7

Main Teachings (II)

The meek don't self-defend — God defends them — Moses stayed quiet and kept working; God's "suddenly" summoned Aaron and Miriam to the tabernacle. God took the offense seriously because division among the three key leaders would have wrecked the work.

Reconciliation precedes answered prayer — Aaron, the high priest, ran to Moses rather than God, knowing God wouldn't hear him until he fixed it with his brother. And the meek man prayed for his attacker — blessing, not cursing.

Meekness is not weakness — the meekest man on earth grabbed a snake by the tail, faced down Pharaoh, parted the Red Sea, and led three million complainers for forty years. Meekness is strength under submission, power under control, the opposite of pride.

Meekness in practice = James 1:19 — swift to hear (eager to listen and understand), slow to speak (not compelled to fire back or have the last word), slow to wrath (nothing in him demands self-defense).

"Let" is the whole assignment — the Word carries all the power it needs to fulfill itself; our part is to stop resisting it. Receiving with meekness means answering every word — forgive, give, serve, pray — with "Okay, Lord, let's do it."

Summary 5 of 7

Principles & Warnings

Pride always has another argument; meekness always has another surrender.

Meekness produces rest; pride produces stress. If the Christian life feels hard, check for pride, not for a harder God.

God is not calling you to be stronger — He's calling you to be softer. The Word needs less resistance, not more power.

The pictures — fire kept in the fireplace; a war horse turned by a small rein: enormous power, fully under control.

Warning — God hears everything — whatever you say to a face or behind a back, God hears, and some words are serious enough to bring His discipline (Miriam's leprosy). Rule: don't say anything about anybody that you don't want God to hear you say.

Warning — aggression wears three masks — open retaliation, passive avoidance, and backbiting are all the same bruised-ego anger, and none of it is meekness.

Warning — prosecution-only verdicts — deciding a matter after hearing one side (often with ourselves as prosecutor, judge, and jury) yields unjust, unmerited, unfair judgments — and most of what we fight over doesn't deserve the attention anyway.

Warning — an unmeek heart blocks the Word — if you can't show meekness to a spouse or fellow believer you can see, you won't show it to God's Word; the Word cannot penetrate a heart that interrupts, argues, and reloads.

Summary 6 of 7

Takeaways & Declarations

Audit your words — before speaking to a face or behind a back, ask: am I comfortable with God hearing this?

Refuse to retaliate — when criticized or wronged, leave it with God like Moses did, and keep doing what God called you to do.

Listen before you judge — keep your mouth shut long enough to hear; ask questions; hear both sides with an open mind before reaching any verdict.

Fix it first — if something stands between you and someone, make the effort at reconciliation before expecting God to receive your gift.

Bring meekness home — "all of us need a good dose of meekness to help our marriages"; stop needing to be right, stop interrupting, surrender the last word.

Stop arguing with the Word — when it says forgive, give, serve, or stop worrying and pray, drop the excuses and say "Okay, Lord, let's do it."

Declarations — "Meekness is not weakness." "Pride always has another argument; meekness always has another surrender." "Meekness produces rest; pride produces stress." "The Word doesn't need more power — it needs less resistance." "Let the word have its way — and it will produce transformation and fruit." And the refrain: "The Word works."
